\begin{abstract}
Forecasting systems are commonly benchmarked by forecast-side scores, yet under deployment frictions these rankings need not preserve deployed operational ranking. We identify a benchmark failure mode: under a fixed deployed interface, the forecast-side winner can recurrently fail to be the deployed winner once friction is moderate or high and proposed actions are not directly realized. \section{Introduction}

A forecasting benchmark is unreliable for deployment if the model that wins on forecast-side scores recurrently fails to be the model that performs best after actions are realized under friction. Forecast-side metrics can be fully appropriate for measuring predictive accuracy, yet they need not be the right object for model selection once forecasts are translated into actions through a frictional interface. This is not a claim that forecast metrics are incorrect; it is a claim that they may be the wrong object for deployment-time model selection under friction. In such settings, forecasts do not directly determine realized outcomes: they first induce proposed actions, which are then transformed by operational rules, adjustment costs, or switching penalties before realized performance is observed.

This paper studies two evaluation questions for forecasting-driven systems under friction. Q1 isolates the mechanism: if the same proposed action path is held fixed, can different interfaces produce different realized outcomes? Q2 asks the benchmark question: under one fixed deployed interface, can forecast-side ranking still be trusted to select the operationally best forecasting system? Forecast-side metrics may remain valid for prediction, yet under a fixed frictional deployment interface they need not be the right object for model selection.

This paper makes three narrow contributions. First, it identifies a benchmark failure mode for forecasting systems under a fixed deployed interface: at moderate-to-high friction, forecast-side ranking can fail to preserve deployed ranking. Second, it shows that this is not merely descriptive ranking drift: in operational settings, moderate-to-high friction can produce recurrent deployed misselection. Third, it separates mechanism (Q1) from benchmark consequence (Q2), clarifying why realized-action evaluation can diverge from forecast-side or target-side quantities and when that divergence matters for model selection. We support this claim with a controlled synthetic anchor, a deliberately small forecasting-native event-micro benchmark, operational corroboration from inventory, and exact-control corroboration from portfolio Q1 support.

\section{Two Evaluation Questions}

Let $\hat{y}_t$ denote a forecast, $a_t^{tar}$ a proposed action derived from it, and $a_t^{exec}$ the realized action after a frictional interface is applied. Let $U(a,y)$ denote realized operational utility against the realized outcome $y_t$. We distinguish three evaluation objects: forecast-side quality, target-side decision quality, and realized-action quality. The benchmark question is which of these objects is appropriate for model selection under deployment constraints.

\noindent\textbf{Q1 (same proposal, different interface).} Holding the proposal fixed at $a_{1:T}^{tar}$, we ask whether varying only the interface that maps proposals to realized actions changes realized operational outcomes. This is an exact-control question: proposal identity must be locked, so any downstream difference must arise from the interface rather than from a different forecast or proposal source.

\noindent\textbf{Q2 (fixed interface, different forecasts).} Q2 is the benchmark-selection question. Holding the interface fixed, we ask whether varying the forecaster changes the relationship between forecast-metric ranking and deployed operational ranking. More directly, does ranking systems by forecast quality still identify the system that is best under deployed realized-action evaluation?

These questions are complementary but not interchangeable. Q1 explains why divergence can arise once realized actions depart from proposed targets, while Q2 asks whether that divergence becomes a benchmark model-selection failure under a fixed deployed interface. Because the two questions change different parts of the pipeline, evidence for one should not be read as automatic evidence for the other.

\section{Evidence Design}

Our evidence hierarchy separates sanity anchoring, forecasting-native main evidence, operational corroboration, and corroborating support. Synthetic provides the zero-friction sanity anchor and controlled ranking-drift evidence. A deliberately small event-micro benchmark provides the main forecasting-native Q2 evidence: models emit event probabilities, a fixed thresholding rule maps probabilities to actions, and switching friction penalizes action changes. Inventory provides operational corroboration, where the same benchmark consequence appears with stronger recurrence magnitudes. Portfolio is used only for exact-control Q1 corroboration, where proposal identity can be locked directly. Load-following remains appendix-only operational support.

In inventory Q2, the locked main table compares five forecasting families under one responsive replenishment rule: Naive persistence, Moving average (7), Linear AR, Small MLP, and Small GRU. The appendix widens that comparison with validation-selected family representatives under the same split, held-out protocol, validation criterion, and standardized two-stage tuning procedure; simple heuristic baselines remain fixed ex ante. The main text keeps the locked five-family comparison, while the appendix records the broader family sweep for baseline-specification transparency.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Q2: Fixed interface, benchmark failure and selection consequence}

Q2 asks the paper's headline benchmark question: under one fixed deployed interface, can forecast-side ranking still be trusted for deployed model selection? Synthetic provides the zero-friction sanity anchor: rankings align exactly at zero friction, and once friction is moderate or high, repeated pairwise flips and lower rank correlation emerge.

Figure~\ref{fig:q2-required} and Table~\ref{tab:event-micro-selection-drift-main} provide the paper's main forecasting-native evidence block. In the event-micro benchmark, agreement is $0.75$ at friction $0.00$, falls to $0.30$ by $0.50$, and reaches $0.05$ at $1.00$. Over the same rows, the deployed winner shifts from Reactive sharp to Calibrated baseline and then Lagged smoother, while deployed suboptimality rises from $5/20$ seeds at zero friction to $14/20$ at $0.50$ and $19/20$ at $1.00$. Tables~\ref{tab:event-micro-threshold-robustness} and \ref{tab:event-micro-logloss-robustness} show that the same qualitative winner-drift and recurrent deployed-suboptimality pattern persists under $\tau = 0.50$ and under log loss.

Table~\ref{tab:q2-selection-drift} remains the paper's main operational benchmark table. Inventory provides operational corroboration with stronger recurrence magnitudes. The friction $0.25$ row is best read as a transition regime: the most frequent deployed winner changes to Linear AR, yet agreement remains $0.70$, deployed suboptimality remains $3/10$, and the median deployed gap remains $0.0$. By friction $0.5$, however, benchmark failure is already recurrent: the forecast-selected model is deployed-suboptimal in $9/10$ seeds, agreement falls to $0.10$, and the median deployed gap reaches $0.227$. At friction $1.0$, deployed suboptimality rises to $10/10$ seeds, with mean and median deployed gaps of $1.187$ and $1.162$. Thus the practical issue is not merely that rankings drift, but that model choice under a fixed deployed rule becomes repeatedly wrong at moderate-to-high friction.

Appendix Tables~\ref{tab:q2-stronger-protocol}--\ref{tab:q2-stronger-robustness} widen the inventory comparison set under a standardized tuning protocol. That broader-family sweep strengthens moderate/high-friction mismatch, but the zero- and low-friction rows remain too mixed for headline use.

\subsection{Q1: Same proposal, different interface}

Q1 is the mechanism section: it explains why the Q2 benchmark failure can arise. In the synthetic benchmark, holding the proposal fixed while varying only the interface yields exact zero-friction coincidence and a proposal-versus-realization gap that rises from $0.000$ at zero friction to $0.223$ at friction $1.0$.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{assets/figures/fig_q1_results_v2.pdf}
\caption{Q1 --- the same proposal can yield different realized outcomes under different interfaces. At zero friction, target-side and realized-side quantities coincide or nearly coincide. Under positive friction, interface-mediated differences emerge. The synthetic benchmark shows the controlled phenomenon cleanly, while the inventory domain shows a threshold pattern: low friction can remain mixed, but sufficiently high friction makes the tempered interface beneficial through reduced adjustment costs. The two panels use different y-axis quantities because the synthetic and inventory Q1 evidence emphasize controlled gap emergence and operational score effects, respectively.}
\label{fig:q1-required}
\end{figure}

Inventory reproduces the same mechanism in an operational setting: zero friction is exact by construction, low friction can remain mixed, and sufficiently high friction makes tempered execution beneficial through lower adjustment costs. Portfolio provides exact-control corroboration only: proposal identity is directly verified, zero-cost behavior is near-flat, and positive-cost improvements recur across universes. Q1 therefore explains why forecast-side, target-side, and realized-action evaluation can diverge, while Q2 shows when that divergence becomes a model-selection problem.

\section{Discussion and Limitations}

The paper's practical claim is narrow: forecast-side metrics may remain valid for prediction, yet under a fixed frictional deployment interface they need not be the right object for model selection. In the forecasting-native event-micro benchmark, that mismatch becomes a recurrent benchmark-selection failure at moderate-to-high friction, and inventory shows the same consequence in an operational domain with stronger recurrence magnitudes. Q1 explains why divergence can arise, and Q2 shows when that divergence matters for model selection.

Three limitations bound the claim. Forecasting-native Q2 evidence comes from a deliberately small event-micro benchmark. Operational corroboration remains concentrated in one inventory domain. In the expanded-family inventory sweep, moderate/high-friction mismatch strengthens but zero- and low-friction rows remain mixed, so we do not use that block as main-text confirmation.

The resulting implication is evaluative rather than predictive: forecasting systems under deployment constraints should be judged with realized-action-aware criteria rather than forecast-side quantities alone.
